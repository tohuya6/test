            <h2>Key Concepts and Equations</h2>


            <h3>Charging Methods</h3>

            <p>Objects gain or lose electric charge through specific processes, each with distinct mechanisms and outcomes:</p>

            <ul>

                <li><strong>Friction:</strong> Electrons transfer between materials due to contact and separation, leaving one object positively charged and the other negatively charged. For example, rubbing a glass rod with silk transfers electrons to the silk, leaving the glass rod positively charged and the silk negatively charged.</li>

                <li><strong>Conduction:</strong> Direct physical contact transfers electrons between objects, typically from a charged object to a conductor or vice versa.</li>

                <li><strong>Induction:</strong> A charged object induces charge separation in a nearby conductor without direct contact.</li>

            </ul>


            <h3>Coulomb's Law</h3>

            <div class="equation">

                Fₑ = k (|q₁q₂| / r²)

            </div>

            <p><strong>Conditions of Use:</strong> Applies to point charges or spherical charge distributions treated as point charges, in a vacuum or air (where k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²), assuming charges are stationary.</p>

            <p><strong>Applications:</strong> Calculate the electrostatic force between two point charges, analyze attraction or repulsion in atomic or molecular systems.</p>


            <h3>Electric Fields</h3>

            <p><strong>Point Charge:</strong></p>

            <div class="equation">

                E = k (|q| / r²)

            </div>

            <p><strong>Continuous Distributions:</strong></p>

            <div class="equation">

                E = (1 / 4πε₀) ∫ (dq / r²)

            </div>
